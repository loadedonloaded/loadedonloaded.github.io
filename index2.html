Bio

I am a postdoctoral lecturer at Penn State, where I study the history of digital culture. My work inquires generally into the value of the humanities—and especially the critical, historicist, and decolonial strains of the humanities—amid the rise of digital media.

My book project, Computation and Critique: Two Cultures of Literary Method, 1945-1990, reconsiders the relationship between literary expertise and computational expertise since WWII. Specifically, it reconsiders the prevailing assumption that there is no such relationship: that while statisticians, linguists, and social scientists have spent decades refining computer-aided methods of textual analysis, literary critics have remained dogged close readers, out of touch and thus out of favor in the so-called digital age. Instead of trying to infuse computational know-how into contemporary literary methodologies, Computation and Critique argues that many of our non-technicist critical practices already represent longstanding engagements with computational methods, ranging from stylostatistics and content analysis to generative grammar and cliometrics. Through case studies of Christine Brooke-Rose, J.M. Coetzee, Henry Louis Gates, Jr., and Clarence Major, I show that some of the definitive developments in postwar literary criticism—and especially developments in feminist, postcolonial, and African American literary studies—offer crucial tools for understanding digital culture. In the process I excavate a counter-history of computational expertise: an expertise grounded not in the training or methodologies of data scientists, but in the anti-racist, anti-sexist, and anti-colonial theories of language and culture that, I argue, constitute the truly valuable intellectual products of the humanities in a digital age.
