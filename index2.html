Bio

I am a postdoctoral lecturer at Penn State, where I study the history of digital culture. My work inquires generally into the value of the humanities—and especially the critical, historicist, and decolonial strains of the humanities—amid the so-called digital age. What does it mean, for instance, to study computers and data from a humanistic perspective? Why should humanists continue to study language when it appears that culture is increasingly inscribed not in novels or poetry, but in numbers and code? These are some of the questions I try to answer through my writing and research.

My book project, Computation and Critique: Two Cultures of Literary Method, 1945-1990, [ARGUES] [CRITICAL METHODS OF READING CAME OUT OF CONTACT WITH DIGITAL METHODS: FEMINIST, POSTCOLONIAL, AFRICAN AMERICAN LITERARY STUDIES] [WHILE THESE CRITICAL METHODS ARE NOT TECHNICIST, AND NOT WHAT WE WOULD DESCRIBE AS DIGITAL, THEY ARE A PART OF THE DIGITAL AGE] [THROUGH CASE STUDIES OF CHRISTINE BROOKE-ROSE, J.M. COETZEE, HENRY LOUIS GATES, JR., AND CLARENCE MAJOR]
